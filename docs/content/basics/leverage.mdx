# Using Leverage

Leverage lets you make a bigger bet than your own cash would allow. You put up
some money, Pred lends you the rest, and your position acts larger than your
deposit.

## A simple example

You have 100 dollars and you are confident about YES. With 3x leverage you
control a 300 dollar position. You put in 100, Pred lends 200.

```ts
yourCash   = 100
borrowed   = 200
position   = 300   // acts 3x larger

// if YES wins, you gain on the full 300, then repay 200 plus interest
// if YES loses, the losses eat your 100 first
```

If YES wins, your upside is on the full 300. If YES loses, the losses come out of
your 100 first, and if you lose enough your position is closed to protect the
loan.

Pred currently allows up to **5x** leverage. There are no separate per-user caps.

## The borrow rate

Borrowing is not free. You pay a borrow rate while the loan is open. The rate is
not fixed. It leans based on how lopsided the market is.

- If lots of people borrow to bet the same side, joining that side costs more.
- Borrowing to bet the other side costs less, sometimes even earns a rebate.

This nudge keeps the two sides roughly balanced, which is what keeps lending
safe. More in [The Lending Machine](/economy/lending-machine).

## Liquidation

If your position loses enough value it gets liquidated, closed early so the loan
is repaid. This happens at around 7% remaining equity. Whatever is left after
repaying the loan and fees comes back to you.

Pred does not liquidate on a hair trigger:

- A position becomes liquidatable only 30 seconds after the oracle update that
  crossed the line.
- Liquidation pauses if a second price source disagrees with the oracle.

This blocks a single bad price print from wiping you out.

## The deleverage cliff

This is the part that makes leverage on a prediction market work at all.

A normal market falls step by step, so a loan can be wound down along the way. A
prediction market does not. At resolution it jumps: YES goes straight to 1 or
straight to 0, with nothing in between. A loan caught in that jump could blow
clean through its collateral.

So Pred closes the borrowed part of every position in the final minutes before
resolution. This is the deleverage cliff.

> After the cliff your debt is zero. You still hold your own bet, just without
> the borrowed money on top. The resolution jump then lands on an ordinary,
> unleveraged position, where it cannot create a bad debt.

You do not have to do anything. The cliff runs automatically per market just
before its deadline.

## In short

- You put up part, Pred lends the rest, up to 5x.
- You pay a borrow rate that leans to keep the book balanced.
- Lose too much and you are liquidated at about 7% equity.
- Just before resolution the borrowed part is closed for you, so the final jump
  never hits a loan.

Why this is safe even in the worst case is the subject of
[The Safety Wall](/economy/safety-wall).
