# How Prediction Markets Work

A prediction market turns a question into a price. That price is the crowd's best
guess at the odds.

## Price is probability

Every share settles at either 1 dollar or 0 dollars. YES pays 1 dollar if the
answer is yes, otherwise nothing. NO is the mirror.

Because a winning share is worth exactly 1 dollar, the price you pay today is
just the market's estimate of the chance.

| Price you pay | What it means |
| --- | --- |
| 0.65 | About a 65% chance |
| 0.50 | A coin flip |
| 0.10 | A long shot, about 10% |

If you buy YES at 0.65 and the answer is yes, you paid 65 cents to receive 1
dollar. Your profit is 35 cents per share. If the answer is no, you lose the 65
cents.

```ts
// buying YES at 0.65
cost   = 0.65
payout = answerIsYes ? 1.00 : 0.00
profit = payout - cost   // +0.35 if yes, -0.65 if no
```

## YES and NO always add to 1

Exactly one side wins, so the fair price of YES and the fair price of NO add up
to 1 dollar. If fair YES is 0.40, fair NO is 0.60.

Pred quotes a little above fair on both sides, so its two prices add up to
slightly more than 1. That small extra is the spread, the bookmaker's margin. See
[What is Pred](/basics/what-is-pred) for the worked example.

## Where the fair price comes from

Pred does not invent the odds. It reads them from an oracle, the pricing layer
inside DeepBook Predict. The oracle publishes a fair probability `f` for each
market and updates it as new information arrives. Pred takes that fair price,
adds its spread, applies a small nudge to keep its book balanced, and shows you
the result. Details are in [How the Economy Works](/economy).

> If the oracle goes stale, meaning its last update is too old to trust, Pred
> stops quoting that market and pauses it, rather than show a price it cannot
> stand behind.

## Resolution

When the deadline hits, the oracle settles the market to yes or no. Winning
shares are redeemed for 1 dollar each, losing shares expire worthless, and your
balance updates. On Pred, any borrowed money is always closed out just before
this moment, which is covered in [Using Leverage](/basics/leverage).
